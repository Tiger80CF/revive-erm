Emerging Risk

AI Data Centers Are Breaking the Grid

Your cloud contract excludes it. Your DR plan inherits that exclusion. Your Business Interruption insurance policy won't pay when the cloud provider invokes force majeure. The cascade is already built into your contracts.

The numbers are staggering. AI model training runs now consume as much electricity as small cities. Hyperscale data centers are signing power purchase agreements that exceed the capacity of regional grids. And the growth curve shows no sign of flattening.

This is creating a new category of enterprise risk that most organizations haven't mapped — and their existing contracts have already locked in the exposure. The gap isn't that three instruments weren't designed to work together. It's that they form a cascade: your cloud contract is the source document, your DR plan derives from its provisions, and your Business Interruption insurance policy won't pay when the cloud provider's exclusions apply.

The Power Demand Problem

Global data center electricity consumption is projected to double by 2028. AI workloads are the primary driver — a single large language model training run can consume more electricity than 100 US households use in a year.

The grid wasn't built for this. Utilities are scrambling to add capacity, but new generation and transmission infrastructure takes years to permit and build. In the meantime, the hyperscalers are competing for every available megawatt.

The result is a growing mismatch between where compute capacity exists and where reliable power can be delivered. Some regions are already seeing constraints. More will follow.

"Your cloud provider's SLA doesn't guarantee the electricity will stay on."

The Cascade

AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud all treat extreme weather and grid events as force majeure — no damages, no SLA-style liability. The language is explicit: "electrical or power outages," "power blackout," "acts of God," "natural disasters." When a climate-driven grid event hits, the cloud provider is contractually protected.

But that's just the first link. Here's how the cascade unfolds:

The Cascade

1. Cloud Contract: AWS, Azure, and GCP all explicitly exclude liability for grid-related outages. The force majeure clauses cover "electrical or power outages, utilities or other telecommunications failures, earthquake, storms or other elements of nature." When the grid fails, your cloud provider invokes the exclusion. Credits — if any — are your sole remedy. Damages are off the table.
2. DR Plan: The failover regions specified in those contracts — and the DR plans derived from contract provisions — are also in stressed markets, or route through one. None of the big three contractually promise automatic cross-region failover when there's region-wide impact due to weather or grid stress. They provide building blocks and some platform-level failover, but the architecture burden sits with you. When your primary region fails, your backup may be competing for the same constrained power.
3. Business Interruption Insurance Policy: Traditional BI coverage assumes physical damage to your property. Cloud outages don't trigger most policies. Even "contingent business interruption" coverage typically requires physical damage to a supplier's property — and a grid constraint isn't damage, it's capacity management. When the cloud provider's force majeure exclusion applies, the BI claim gets denied.

The cloud provider invokes the exclusion. Failover fails because the backup region is also constrained. The BI claim gets denied because there's no covered damage. The DR plan is worthless.

The Geography

This isn't abstract. The stressed grid markets are PJM, MISO, and ERCOT — which means the cities at risk include New York, Boston, Philadelphia, DC, Chicago, Minneapolis, and Dallas-Fort Worth. The major cloud regions serving these markets share infrastructure dependencies. A heat dome, wildfire taking out transmission, or winter storm doesn't respect availability zone boundaries.

The Timing Problem

This isn't a 2030 problem. The Swiss Re sigma report from March 2026 documents constraints already emerging in key data center markets. Contract renewal cycles are typically annual. Insurance policies renew annually. The window to address the gap before it materializes is closing.

Most enterprises will discover this exposure the hard way — when an outage occurs and they learn what their contracts actually say. The sophisticated ones are mapping the gap now, while they still have leverage to negotiate.

What to Do About It

Audit your cloud contracts. Find the force majeure language. Understand exactly what triggers your SLA credits and what voids them. Most enterprises haven't read this section since they signed.

Review your Business Interruption insurance policy with fresh eyes. Specifically ask your broker: "If our cloud provider experiences a multi-day outage due to grid constraints — no physical damage, just insufficient power — what's covered?" Get the answer in writing.

Stress-test your BCP/DR. Model a scenario where your primary and backup regions both face power constraints simultaneously. Where does that leave you? What's the actual recovery path?

Consider parametric coverage. Some insurers are developing products that trigger based on measurable events (grid frequency deviation, wholesale power prices) rather than traditional damage-based triggers. These may fill gaps that conventional Business Interruption insurance can't reach.

Build the board case. This is an emerging risk that most boards haven't considered. The risk committee should understand the exposure before it materializes. Waiting until after an incident is too late to negotiate better terms.

The enterprises that address this gap in 2026 will have leverage. The ones that wait until 2027 will be renegotiating from a position of weakness — or explaining to their board why they didn't see it coming.

The Bottom Line

The contracts, policies, and plans weren't written to work together — but they do work together, as a cascade that leaves you exposed. The cloud contract is the source document. The DR plan inherits its limitations. The BI policy fails when the cloud exclusion triggers.

The question isn't whether grid constraints will affect enterprise operations. The question is whether you'll discover the cascade before or after it's tested.
